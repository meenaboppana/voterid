The Ballot's In: The Impact of Voter ID Laws on Voter Turnout and Registration

About This Project

Our project examines the impact voter ID laws have on voter registration and turnout within the United States. In particular, we look at impacts across different demographic groups in different states. Using our data, we then create and test a predictive model to estimate the effects of stricter voter ID laws on voter turnout in future elections in certain states.

Types of Voter ID Laws

It turns out not all voter id laws are created equal. A standard division splits these laws into two groups: "non-strict laws" have provisions that sometimes allow voters without identification to cast ballots while "strict laws" require all voters to present ID within a certain timeframe. We scoured the internet to find information on when states implemented these laws. Note that our model does not capture differences in photo ID versus non-photo ID requirements (each of these types can also be either strict or non-strict). Currently, 20 states have no voter ID laws, 22 states have non-strict laws, and 9 states have strict laws. Voter ID laws have been on an upwards trend; no state has, as of today, successfully transitioned from a stricter to a less strict system. (Image source: National Conference of State Legislatures)

Why It Matters

Over the past decade, the implementation of voter identification laws has become increasingly more prevalent -- and more controversial. The 2014 US midterm elections and recent changes in voter ID legislation bring particular relevance to this issue. Thought supporters claim that such laws protect the voting process, many opponents say they decrease voter turnout, especially among minority groups. However, there has been an absence of conclusive studies on this phenomenon. Our project aims to shed some insight on this question through exploratory data analysis.

How We Did It

Our project had one major goal: to investigate the relationship between voter turnout and the existence of voter ID laws on a state-wide level. We started with the hypothesis that voter ID laws might impact members of various ethnic groups differently. We began our analysis by looking at historical turnout data for November elections from 2002 to 2012.

Data

We drew our data on voter registration and turnout from US Census data broken down by state, year, ethnicity, and other factors. This data was available for elections between 2002 and 2012, for a total of 6 election years. Determining voter ID law status on a state by state level required us to draw from several separate data sources, as it was necessary to know the exact description of the law for our strictness classification as well as the exact date of enactment to determine which elections were affected. To check for sources of bias, we also ultimately looked at the margin of victory in each state in the years tested -- we have only examined this for presidential election years, however.

Competitiveness

One of the confounding effects we investigated was whether a race in any particular state was more competitive than any other. For example, a very close race would be likely to increase voter turnout, regardless of other factors. Thus, we also found information on how competitive elections typically are in various states. More competitive elections can increase turnout, independent of voter ID laws. It appears that having strict voter ID laws means a state is more likely to have a competitive election (margin of victory within 10 percent). In averages, states with strict voter ID laws are more likely to have elections with small margins of victory than states with nonstrict laws or no laws at all. Also, as a group they are more likely to have slightly lower turnout. Similarly, states with nonstrict laws have more competitive elections than those without any type of voter ID laws. However, their voter turnout tends to be more comparable to that of states without voter ID laws. In terms of trends, over the past three presidential elections the margin of victory has increased for every subgroup except those without voter ID laws. Additionally, voter turnout has stayed constant or decreased for every subgroup. However, these results are likely caused by some combination of 1) variability between certain elections (an exciting 2008 election, for example) and 2) states changing their voter ID law status. Predictive Model

Using a method called difference-in-differences, we were able to predict the average deviation in voter turnout from the average after a state implements a strict or non strict voter ID law. We found that in most cases voter turnout does tend to decrease slightly after a law is implemented.

Predicting Outcomes in North Dakota, Mississippi, and Texas

We used our model to predict voter turnout in North Dakota, Mississippi, and Texas 2014 elections, all three of which have recently implemented voter ID laws in the past 2 years. We compared our predictions to actual turnout data. While overall turnout data has been released, turnout data for each ethnic group has not, so we were not able to compare those results with our predictions. In Mississippi and Texas, we were within 2 percentage points using this analysis alone, though our prediction was off for North Dakota. (We note that North Dakota was also an outlier in this year's election cycle, with the second to highest voter turnout rate.)

Further Research

A future direction of our research would be to test our model's predictive power of turnout by ethnic group, rather than overall turnout alone. We would be interested to compare our predictions for turnout by ethnic group in North Dakota, Mississippi, and Texas with real data as soon as it becomes available.

Additionally, we hope to integrate the competitiveness of a state into our predictive model for voter turnout. This would help account for inter-state differences and predict future elections more accurately.

A final further direction would be to perform a similar analysis of the effect of voter turnout on other demographics, especially age groups. This would require the exact same statistical methods with some more data wrangling (which we began to do). Other interesting demographics would be to look at gender differences, as well as socioeconomic status.
